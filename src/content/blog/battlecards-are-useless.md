Ask a sales rep when they last opened the competitive battlecard you spent three weeks building. Watch their face. That pause is the whole problem.

Most battlecards die the same death: they're built by marketing, for marketing, and read like an internal wiki. Forty rows of feature comparison, a SWOT nobody asked for, and pricing that went stale two quarters ago. Reps skim it once during onboarding and never again. Meanwhile, in the actual deal, they wing it.

Here's how to build one they'll actually pull up mid-call.

## 1. Build it for the conversation, not the archive

A battlecard is not a research document. It's a **conversation weapon.** The question it answers isn't "everything true about Competitor X" - it's "the prospect just said they're also evaluating X, what do I say in the next ninety seconds?"

Reorganize around live moments:
- What to say when they bring up the competitor
- The two or three traps to set early (questions that expose the competitor's weakness before it comes up)
- The landmines to defuse (where *you* are weak, and the honest reframe)

If a line doesn't help a rep in a live conversation, cut it.

## 2. Cover the deals you actually lose, not every logo

Teams love a battlecard wall with fifteen competitors. It looks thorough. It's useless. Your reps face the same two or three alternatives in 80% of contested deals - and one of those "competitors" is almost always **"do nothing"** or **"build it in-house."**

Go deep on the handful that actually show up. Delete the rest. A great card on your top three beats a mediocre card on fifteen every time.

> The most common competitor in every deal is inertia. If your battlecard has no answer for "we'll just keep doing what we do now," it's missing the deal you lose most.

## 3. Make it one screen

If the rep has to scroll, they've already tabbed back to the call. The usable battlecard fits on a single screen: the competitor, the one-line positioning against them, three traps, three landmines, and the killer proof point. Everything else lives in a linked appendix they'll read on their own time (they won't, and that's fine).

## 4. Source it from won and lost deals, not the competitor's website

Marketing tends to build battlecards by reading the competitor's marketing. That's how you end up arguing against their *messaging* instead of their *reality*. The gold is in your own deal data: win/loss interviews, lost-deal notes, the specific sentence a prospect said right before they chose the other guy. Quote real objections. Reps trust a card that sounds like their actual calls.

## 5. Give it an expiry date

Competitive intel rots. Pricing changes, they ship a feature that kills your favorite talking point, a new player enters. A battlecard with no owner and no review cadence is actively dangerous - a rep confidently repeating a fact that stopped being true in March. Put a "last verified" date on every card and a name next to it. If it hasn't been touched in a quarter, it's a liability, not an asset.

## The test

Here's the only metric that matters: **do reps open it in live deals, unprompted?** Not in training. Not because you nagged them in Slack. In the moment, because it helps them win.

If yes, you have a battlecard. If no, you have a very well-researched document that everyone politely ignores. Rebuild it around the conversation, ruthlessly cut, and keep it alive. That's the difference between competitive enablement and competitive theater.
