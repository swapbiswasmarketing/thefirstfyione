The weekly filing. Five things moving in go-to-market right now, why each matters, and what to do about it. No fluff. Let's go.

## 01 — PLG teams are quietly re-adding sales

The pattern: a wave of product-led companies that spent years insisting "no sales team" are now hiring account executives - just pointing them at self-serve accounts that already have traction. Not a reversal of PLG. An evolution of it.

**Why it matters:** "product-led" was never meant to mean "sales-forbidden." The winning motion is product-led *pipeline* - let the product qualify, then let a human close the expansion. If you run PLG, the interesting question isn't whether to add sales, it's *at what usage signal* you hand an account to one.

**Do this:** define the single product-qualified-lead threshold that predicts expansion best, and route those accounts to a human. One threshold. Start there.

## 02 — Pricing pages are going on a diet

More companies are collapsing five-tier pricing pages down to two or three. The maximalist "compare 40 features across 5 plans" grid is quietly dying.

**Why it matters:** choice paralysis is a conversion killer. Every extra column is a decision you've outsourced to a stranger who wanted to give you money five minutes ago. Fewer, clearer tiers convert better and cut the "which plan is right for me?" support tickets.

**Do this:** look at which plan actually closes most of your revenue. If two tiers carry 90% of it, ask what the other three are really doing besides adding friction.

## 03 — The launch tier is back

Teams are re-adopting **tiered launches** - sorting releases into T1 (company-wide moment), T2 (segment campaign), and T3 (changelog + in-app) instead of treating every ship like a Super Bowl.

**Why it matters:** launching everything at maximum volume trains your audience to ignore you and burns out your team. Tiering restores signal. A T1 lands harder precisely because you didn't scream about the T3 tooltip update.

**Do this:** write down your tier definitions before the next launch calendar planning meeting. The fights about "is this a big deal?" resolve themselves once the criteria exist on paper.

## 04 — "Category creation" is losing to "category clarity"

The mood is shifting. Fewer startups are trying to invent a brand-new category from scratch; more are winning by being the *clearest* option inside an existing one buyers already understand.

**Why it matters:** creating a category is the most expensive move in marketing - you pay to educate the entire market, and usually a fast follower harvests it. For most companies, being obviously the sharpest choice in a category buyers already have budget for is faster, cheaper, and more winnable.

**Do this:** before you coin a new category name, run the honest test - do buyers have a budget line for the problem today? If yes, win the category that exists.

## 05 — Win/loss is becoming continuous, not quarterly

Instead of a big quarterly win/loss study that lands as a PDF nobody reads, teams are running lightweight, always-on loss interviews and piping the themes straight to product and messaging.

**Why it matters:** competitive and messaging intel has a short shelf life. A quarterly snapshot is already stale when it ships. A steady trickle of "here's the exact reason we lost this one" is worth more than a beautiful deck twice a year.

**Do this:** ask every closed-lost owner one question within 48 hours - "what was the real reason?" - and dump the answers somewhere the whole team can see. That's it. That's the program.
