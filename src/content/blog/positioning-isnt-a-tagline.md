Someone on the team says "we need to nail our positioning," and three days later a doc appears with five tagline options in a font nobody approved. This happens everywhere, and it's a category error. A tagline is words on the homepage. Positioning is the thing that decides whether those words make you look inevitable or make you look confusing.

Get this wrong and no amount of clever copy saves you. Get it right and mediocre copy still converts.

## Positioning is context, not a slogan

The clearest way to think about it comes from April Dunford's *Obviously Awesome*: positioning is the **context you set for your product** so that its value is obvious to the right person. It's the frame of reference - what you're compared to, who it's for, what makes you different, and why that difference matters.

Context is doing invisible work at all times. The same bottle of wine tastes more expensive with a French label. The same product looks like a bargain next to enterprise software and a rip-off next to a free tool. You don't get to opt out of context - your prospect will slot you into *some* mental category the second they land. Positioning is you choosing that category on purpose, instead of letting them guess.

> A tagline is an output. Positioning is the input that makes the tagline possible. Skip the input and you're just decorating a fog.

## The five pieces (that come before any copy)

Before you write a single word of headline, you need answers to these:

1. **Competitive alternatives.** What would a customer do if you didn't exist? (Often a spreadsheet, a manual process, or a competitor - and it's rarely what you assume.)
2. **Unique attributes.** What do you have that the alternatives don't? Features, yes, but also model, data, integrations, ecosystem.
3. **Value.** So what? What can the customer *do* because of those attributes that they couldn't before? Attributes are boring; value is the point.
4. **Who cares most.** Which customers find that value urgent enough to buy now? This is your best-fit segment, not your total market.
5. **Market category.** What context makes your value instantly obvious? The category sets expectations for price, features, and competitors.

Notice that "tagline" appears nowhere. The tagline is what you write *after* these five are locked. If you're arguing about slogans before you've agreed on your competitive alternatives, you're arranging furniture in a house with no foundation.

## The test for weak positioning

Weak positioning has a tell: **your prospects keep misunderstanding what you are.** Symptoms:

- Sales calls open with ten minutes of "so, wait, are you like [wrong competitor]?"
- Deals stall because the buyer compared you on the wrong criteria (they benchmarked your premium product against a free tool).
- Your best customers describe what you do better than your homepage does.

That last one is the real diagnostic. Go interview five customers who love you and ask what they'd have Googled to find you and what they almost bought instead. Their answers are your positioning, sitting in plain sight. Most companies are positioned *by accident* by their happiest users - the work is to notice it and make it deliberate.

## Where teams go wrong

Two failure modes dominate.

**Positioning by feature list.** You lead with what you built instead of the frame that makes it matter. Buyers don't experience features; they experience whether you're obviously right for them or vaguely maybe.

**Positioning for the whole market.** You try to be legible to everyone and end up sharp to no one. Strong positioning deliberately makes some people bounce - because it makes exactly the right people lean in.

Fix the context first. The tagline will practically write itself, and for the first time it'll be pointing at something real. Strictly FYI.
